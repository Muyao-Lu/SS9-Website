Breaking News - Seven Years War Comes to an End!

After a long and arduous war spanning from 1756 to 1763, humanity has finally decided to stop killing itself after the signing of the Treaty of Paris. This long and bloody conflict which was beginning to become boring for our human politics department to analyze has finally led to victory on the side of the English and Prussians, leading to:

  • The relinquishment of French colonies in North America to Britain
  • The loss of French strongholds on the Indian Subcontinent, giving Great Britan control of "All of North America east of the Mississippi" (Wikipedia)
  • Bankruptcy of both England and France, which eventually caused tax increases in British colonies and widespread discontent leading to the American Revolution
  • Transfer of lands between Britan, France, and Spain
  • Rising tensions between native inhabitants and colonizers of North America, brought about by the differing attitudes of the British and French towards First Nations
  • Canada becoming a British Colony

Special Notice: Due to these concequences of the war, Hamster Tourism LTD© will now be discontinuing boat trips to North America and the Indian Subcontinent until further notice. We apologise for the inconvenience.

The Map Zone

During this war, many lands were exchanged between the French and British, with battles being fought over vast expanses of North America, Europe, and India. With these tremendous changes in the geography, our map department has decided to create a map of North America, demonstrating the changes. Before the war, the continent was split between the Spanish, French, and British. However, as a concequence of the Treaty of Paris, the French had to cede their land (dark gray) to the British, resulting in them losing most of their foothold in North America.

Elsewhere, in Europe and India, less dramatic changes occured. In India, the French were forced to destroy most of their forts, meanwhile, in Europe, the largest changes were not geographical, but political, with many of the participating countries bankrupting themselves through the long and arduous war.

In terms of battles, the ones with the highest casualties were mainly fought in Europe, with significant battles also being fought in both India and North America (mainly the area around the 13 Colonies). One highly decisive North American battle that was fought between the French and British was the seige of Quebec and the subsequent Battle of the Plains of Abraham. Occuring in French Canada, this battle saw generals Montcalm and Wolfe face off against each other. Both men perished in battle, with the British gaining the city of Quebec.

Causes

Though this war was incited for a number of reasons, and saw it's roots in the War of Austrian Succession, the catalyzing event that elevated this war to these heights was conflicts between the British and French over colonies in North America. To delve deeper, as well as to answer any unanswered questions, we invited our resident Human Expert: Dr. Fluffy

The Human Corner, presented by Dr. Fluffy

Q: Why do you think that the British and French, as two superpowers representing the entire human species, would go to a war on this scale? Are humans not as intelligent as we think they are?

A: Though humans are an intelligent species, they aren't perfectly rational. In a rational world, the two nations would have solved their disputes via means of negotiation. However, when feelings such as anger and fear are taken into account - especially when money and resources are involved (in the case of the British and the French, as their fighting was centered around colonies and trade routes), or life itself as with the case of the First Nations, the human species can and will put up quite a fight. The pitting of traditional enemies exacerbated this even further, with the Austrians and Prussians as well as French and English both being enemies for hundreds of years.
- Dr. Fluffy

Q: In terms of weapons, are there any concerning developments in human tech that could threaten hamsterkind?

A: Not particularly. Though this war demonstrated the effictiveness of a strong navy and firearms such as flintlock pistols and muskets, they have yet to figure out stronger guns, betting entire battles on the fact that their powder doesn't get wet. Sabers and bayonets were also used, but those have been around since the Middle Ages, and don't seem to be going away anytime soon. In addition, the humans still have not figured out the fact that standing and shooting at each other in organized linups is not the best way to fight, leaving hamster experts puzzled.
- Cheers, Dr. Fluffy

Q: What will the war mean for humanity? How about hamsterkind?

A: Tough question, however, it is forcasted that both the British and French economies will take a nosedive as they spent quite a bit of money on fighting this war. Increased oppression of First Nations and other French allies in North America will also likely occur, as the French alliesx seem to be too concerned about their own failing economy to be compassionate with anyone right now. Finally, I'm also hearing reports about the French strengthening their military, so that could mean another war (hopefully not). As for hamsters, I would recommend all individuals reading this now to maintain a low profile. The last thing we need is an angry mob of post-war humans coming after us and the less attention right now, the better.
- Dr. Fluffy
